Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Juntong Ye - juy037@ucsd.edu

Proposition:After Missouri's clinics plunged by 67% (2017-2020), its neighbors saw abortion rates surge well above the U.S. average

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Truncate Y-Axis at 0 (Score: -1.0)
    By hiding the negative range, the ~20% vs. ~2% gap looks far more dramatic, exaggerating the perceived difference.
  • Exact Value Annotations (Score: +1.0)
    Labeling each bar with its percentage removes guesswork and builds trust by clarifying the data.
  • Declarative, Leading Title (Score: -0.5)
    The phrase "Spillover Surge" frames the chart as proof of the effect before inspection, priming the reader's interpretation.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Area-Normalized Density (Score: +1.5)
    Normalizing counts to density compensates for the small neighbor sample, revealing overlapping distribution shapes.
  • Identical Bin Breaks (Score: +1.0)
    Using fixed, regular intervals ensures neither group gains an unfair visual advantage from arbitrary bin edges.
  • Semi-Transparent, Matching Palette (Score: +1.0)
    Equal opacity and hue for both curves convey parity, preventing any group from visually popping.
  • Neutral, Descriptive Title & Labels (Score: +1.0)
    A straightforward title and axis labels avoid editorializing, letting the data overlap speak for itself.

Final Reflection

Designing the bar chart was straightforward. It follows the standard approach of comparing two average values. The real challenge was choosing which conventions to bend. Truncating the vertical axis amplifies the difference dramatically but removes important context. By contrast, creating the normalized histogram demanded a more nuanced approach. I had to adjust bin widths, scale the axis, and manage color transparency so that the neighbor distribution aligned clearly with the national pattern without making the chart look dull. This process showed that small default settings such as axis starting point, bin definition, and color intensity can greatly influence how readers understand the data.

What surprised me most was how subtle design choices can steer a viewer's perception. The same data can either highlight an effect or hide it entirely depending on a handful of settings. This insight has refined my view of ethical visualization. Choosing accurate data is only the first step. It is also critical to disclose transformations such as axis truncation or aggregation methods and to examine if each design decision serves genuine understanding or simply reinforces a preferred storyline. In practice, responsible persuasion means presenting real patterns with honesty. Misleading persuasion comes from obscuring variability or exaggerating trends in ways that readers will not immediately notice. When in doubt, I now choose transparency by using clear labels, full data ranges, and neutral color schemes instead of decorative extras.
